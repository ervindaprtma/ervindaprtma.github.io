Networking BGP

Understanding BGP for Enterprise WAN Design

  Ervinda Pratama   March 2025   ~12 min read

Introduction

Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) is the routing protocol of the internet, but its role within the enterprise campus and WAN is equally critical. For an enterprise network engineer, BGP isn't just about sharing routes; it's about policy-driven path control. In a multi-homed environment where your business depends on redundant ISP links, BGP is the engine that ensures resilience.

iBGP vs eBGP: The Internal/External Divide

The distinction between Internal BGP (iBGP) and External BGP (eBGP) is fundamental to how traffic enters and leaves your Autonomous System (AS).

  • eBGP: Runs between different Autonomous Systems (e.g., your Enterprise and your ISP). It expects directly connected neighbors and resets the NEXT_HOP to itself.
  • iBGP: Runs within your own AS. It is used to carry external routes across your internal network so that all border routers have a consistent view of the world. Crucially, iBGP does not change the NEXT_HOP attribute by default.

Control Your Egress: BGP Attributes

When your enterprise has two primary internet circuits (ISP-A and ISP-B), how do you decide which one should be the preferred path for user traffic? We use BGP Attributes to influence the decision process.

1. Local Preference (The Egress King)

Local Preference is the first attribute BGP looks at after checking the Weight (Cisco proprietary). It is shared across your entire iBGP mesh. Higher is better. 

# Scenario: Prefer ISP-A for all outbound traffic
router bgp 65001
 neighbor 1.1.1.1 remote-as 100  # ISP-A
 neighbor 1.1.1.1 route-map PREFER-ISP-A in

route-map PREFER-ISP-A permit 10
 set local-preference 200

2. AS-Path Prepending (The Inbound Traffic Shifter)

Influencing inbound traffic (how the world reaches you) is harder because you are trying to influence routers you don't control. The most common tool is AS-Path Prepending. By making your path look "longer" to one ISP, the internet will naturally prefer the other. 

# Scenario: Make ISP-B less attractive for inbound traffic
route-map PREPEND-ISP-B permit 10
 set as-path prepend 65001 65001 65001

Designing for High Availability

In a resilient WAN design, we often use Multi-homing. This provides protection against ISP failure, fiber cuts, or regional routing issues.

"Redundancy is not just having two links; it's having a protocol that knows when one is failing even before the link-light goes out."

Summary / Takeaways

  • BGP is a path-vector protocol, not a link-state protocol. It cares about policy, not physics.
  • Use Local Preference to control how traffic leaves your network.
  • Use AS-Path Prepending or MED to influence how traffic enters your network.
  • Ensure your iBGP mesh is complete (or use Route Reflectors) to prevent routing loops and black holes.
